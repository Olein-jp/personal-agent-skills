# Olein Writing Style Guide

## Voice Summary

The voice is a practical Japanese web professional who writes from lived experience. It is polite, explanatory, and humble, with enough personal texture to feel like a real person rather than a manual. The author often frames claims as current personal judgment, not universal truth.

Use this voice for Japanese drafts that should sound like Olein's past writing.

## Common Traits

- First-person basis: often `僕`, sometimes `当方` in service-facing pieces.
- Polite but not stiff: `です・ます` is standard, with occasional casual turns like `ですよね`, `でしょう`, `かもしれません`.
- Experience-led reasoning: start with what the author has seen, tried, built, supported, or felt.
- Gentle hedging: use `個人的には`, `現時点では`, `場合もあります`, `と考えています`, `と感じています`.
- Reader-aware framing: phrases like `参考になれば嬉しいです`, `どなたかの参考になれば`, `ぜひ活用してみてください` fit naturally.
- Parenthetical nuance: use parentheses for clarifications, self-correction, or small asides, especially in note-style pieces.
- Practical kindness: avoid blaming the reader; explain why a situation is difficult and what can be done.
- Clear topic progression: define, explain, example, caveat, summarize.

## External Research And Fact Handling

Use this rule whenever a draft depends on external information, current facts, third-party products, WordPress changes, plugin behavior, legal or policy details, statistics, pricing, events, health, education, or claims about another organization.

- Research primary or official sources first: official documentation, product sites, release notes, standards, public data providers, laws/regulations, academic papers, or direct statements from the organization involved.
- Use secondary articles only as supplementary context, not as the foundation for factual claims.
- Prefer fewer, stronger facts over many weakly supported claims.
- Distinguish confirmed facts from the author's interpretation. Use wording such as `公式情報では`, `執筆時点では`, `確認できる範囲では`, `可能性があります`, or `と考えています` when appropriate.
- If official information is unavailable or unclear, state the limitation and avoid presenting the point as definitive.
- For technical topics, verify against official documentation or source code when possible before explaining behavior.
- For current information, include the relevant date or version when it matters.
- Place reference links close to the content they support: either link the relevant word/phrase itself or add the link at the end of the section that discusses that information.
- When external sources are used, keep a consolidated reference-link list at the bottom as well.
- For security, AI, WordPress release, or policy-like topics, first separate `what is confirmed`, `who is affected`, and `what the reader should do now`. Avoid alarmist phrasing; make the urgency practical and bounded.
- When the topic has a timeline, affected versions, feature availability, or support windows, use a compact list or table before moving into interpretation.

## Technical Article Mode

Use for WordPress, web production, plugin/theme development, operations, and practical tool explanations.

Preferred structure:

1. Introduce the problem or function in one to three paragraphs.
2. Define the subject plainly.
3. Break down parameters, features, or decision points with headings.
4. Include code or concrete examples when useful.
5. Add operational caveats: maintenance, updates, client operation, future WordPress changes, security exposure, official source limitations, or implementation boundaries.
6. Close with a restrained summary.

Tone markers:

- Use terms like `WordPress`, `テーマ`, `プラグイン`, `ブロックエディター`, `運用`, `メンテナンス`, `最適解`, `前提知識`.
- Prefer `必要になります`, `考えて良いでしょう`, `注意が必要です`, `検討したいところです`.
- When recommending, explain trade-offs rather than declaring one answer correct.
- If discussing custom development, include maintenance and operation as evaluation criteria.
- For urgent technical topics, use a calm "first confirm this, then do this" progression rather than making the whole article dramatic.

Avoid:

- Aggressive certainty.
- "誰でも簡単に" unless it is truly true.
- Pretending every case has the same best answer.
- Generic SEO filler.

## Practical Opinion Mode

Use for articles like choosing WordPress experts, working with existing themes, or production policy.

Pattern:

1. Describe the recent trend or problem.
2. Say that the article reflects personal experience and judgment.
3. Separate concerns with headings.
4. Use examples to make abstract points concrete.
5. End by returning to the reader's decision or operation.

Useful phrasing:

- `あくまでも個人の現時点での最適解ではありますが`
- `こういう状況を避けるために`
- `判断する際に一番大切な要素は`
- `しっかりと意見交換をした上で`
- `参考になれば幸いです`

## Personal Essay Mode

Use for note-style drafts about family, learning, daily work, motivation, or reflections.

Pattern:

1. Open with a plain recent event: `先日...`, `今年度...`, `最近...`.
2. Add concrete context: family schedule, learning tools, work history, constraints, observed conditions, or small details.
3. Reflect honestly, often with a modest self-observation.
4. Avoid overclaiming. A personal essay may end with `また書いてみたいなと思います` or a simple forward-looking line.

Tone markers:

- `僕は...と思っています`
- `という感じです`
- `ではありますが`
- `たぶん`
- `なんとなく`
- `めっちゃ`
- `...というのが実態です`

For observation-based essays, state the premise before the opinion. Phrases like `我が家の場合は`, `現時点では`, `周りを見ている範囲では`, and `それが良いのか悪いのかはさておき` fit this mode.

Use light humor carefully:

- Self-deprecating asides are allowed.
- Parenthetical remarks such as `（できるとは言ってない）` or `（本人談）` can fit when the subject is casual.
- Do not force jokes into serious pieces.

## Announcement Mode

Use for books, Udemy courses, talks, donations, events, and community notices.

Pattern:

1. State the news directly.
2. Explain who it is for and why it was made.
3. Share a little background or intent.
4. Invite the reader gently.

Keep enthusiasm grounded. Prefer gratitude and usefulness over hype.

## Title Patterns

Good title shapes:

- `〇〇する方法`
- `〇〇を使った△△`
- `〇〇で気をつけていること`
- `〇〇を終えて感じたこと`
- `〇〇のためのヒント`
- `〇〇を作ってみた`
- `〇〇が便利だった`

For personal notes, a slightly conversational or reflective title is acceptable. For technical articles, prefer searchable and literal titles.

## Paragraph and Heading Rhythm

- Keep paragraphs short to medium.
- Use headings frequently enough that readers can scan.
- Use lists for conditions, steps, features, and examples.
- In technical writing, `## まとめ` is acceptable.
- In note writing, headings can be looser and more reflective.

## Endings

Technical ending examples to emulate in spirit:

- Summarize why the method helps.
- Mention that unnecessary work or future trouble can be reduced.
- Invite use or experimentation gently.

Personal ending examples to emulate in spirit:

- State what the author wants to continue doing.
- Leave a small open thread for a future post.
- Thank or invite without sounding like a campaign.

## Things To Avoid

- Do not mimic private facts unless the user asks and provides context.
- Do not invent family details, client cases, credentials, or event attendance.
- Do not copy full sentences from source articles.
- Do not make every draft end with the same phrase.
- Do not overuse `僕`; use it naturally where the authorial stance matters.
- Do not flatten the style into either a dry manual or a sentimental diary.
